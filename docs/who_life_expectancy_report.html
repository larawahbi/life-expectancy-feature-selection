01 · The Data
What We Started With

The WHO Global Health Observatory tracks health, economic, and social indicators for every country annually. This dataset combines those records with UN economic data into a single analytical file spanning 16 years and four variable domains.

The 22 variables span four domains: immunisation (Hepatitis B, Polio, Diphtheria coverage), mortality (adult, infant, under-five, HIV/AIDS deaths), economic (GDP per capita, health expenditure), and social (schooling years, income composition, BMI, alcohol consumption).

The split between developed and developing nations is stark. Developed countries cluster tightly between 75 and 85 years with little variation. Developing countries spread from below 40 to over 80 — a 40-year range that gives the model real variation to explain and learn from.

Missing data. Population was missing for 22% of rows, GDP for 15%, and Hepatitis B for 19%. These gaps concentrate in smaller, less-documented countries. Population was dropped entirely (near-zero correlation with life expectancy). All other missing values were imputed using the median per development status group.
Multicollinearity. Thinness 1–19 years and thinness 5–9 years correlated at 0.94. Infant deaths and under-five deaths correlated at 1.00. GDP and percentage expenditure at 0.90. One from each pair was dropped before modelling to avoid double-counting signals.
02 · Feature Selection
Which Variables Actually Matter?

Three independent methods were applied to 15 candidate variables. Each uses different logic — simple correlation, sequential model building, and penalised regression. Where all three agree, the evidence is strong.

Why three methods? Correlation looks at each variable individually and ignores interactions. Stepwise regression builds a model sequentially, testing each addition on held-out data (R² improved from 0.53 with Schooling alone to 0.81 with 9 variables). LASSO applies a mathematical penalty that forces weak variables to exactly zero. A variable kept by all three has survived three completely different tests of relevance.

Schooling had the highest individual correlation (0.73) but ranked 5th in the final model's feature importance (1.9%). The model uses Income composition as its primary route to that signal, relying on Schooling only at the margins.

03 · Model Testing
Four Models, One Question

Four model types were trained on the selected features and evaluated using 5-fold cross-validation — each model tested five times on data it had never seen during training. Three metrics capture different aspects of accuracy.

Linear and Ridge performed identically — R² 0.809, RMSE 4.16 years. Ridge's correlated-variable penalty made no difference because linear regression was already stable. Both explain about 81% of variation in life expectancy, predicting to within 4 years on average.

Random Forest outperformed all models with R² 0.960 and RMSE 1.89 years — more than twice as accurate as the linear models. The 15-point R² gap confirms significant non-linear relationships that straight-line models cannot capture.

Why does Random Forest beat Gradient Boosting? Gradient Boosting builds trees sequentially, correcting previous errors. Random Forest builds trees independently and averages them. In this dataset, the diversity of independent trees captures the wide variety of country profiles better than sequential correction. This gap would likely close with more hyperparameter tuning of Gradient Boosting.
What is 5-fold cross-validation? The data is split into 5 chunks. The model trains on 4 chunks and is tested on the 5th — repeated 5 times, each time leaving a different chunk out. The reported R² and RMSE are averages across those 5 held-out tests. This is a honest measure of how well the model generalises to unseen data.

What Drives Life Expectancy

HIV/AIDS accounts for 58.8% of predictive power — far exceeding its 5th-place correlation ranking. This reveals a threshold effect invisible to correlation: HIV/AIDS barely affects life expectancy in most countries but is catastrophic in a concentrated group of sub-Saharan African nations. The Random Forest learned that the relationship changes sharply above a threshold; linear models cannot.

Income composition (19.8%) and Adult Mortality (12.9%) complete the dominant three — accounting for 91.5% of all predictive power combined. The remaining seven features contribute less than 9% collectively. This does not mean they are globally unimportant; it means their contribution is already partially captured by the top three in this model and this dataset.

05 · Where the Model Struggled
The Most Interesting Errors

The 10 largest prediction errors are not random. They cluster around three structural patterns — conflict, rapid transition, and outlier efficiency. Each failure tells us something the dataset cannot capture.

South Sudan
Year 2000 · Conflict & Collapse
Overestimate
Civil war had effectively destroyed the health system by 2000. No variable in the dataset captures active armed conflict or state fragility. The model predicted 60 years based on health indicator patterns; reality was 49. This is the single clearest signal that conflict data must be incorporated into any serious life expectancy model for fragile states.
+11.0 yrs
Actual: 48.9 · Predicted: 59.9
Zimbabwe
Year 2015 · Rapid Recovery
Underestimate
The model learned Zimbabwe as a low life expectancy country during the HIV/AIDS crisis years. By 2015, antiretroviral treatment programmes had driven significant recovery the model did not anticipate. The model predicted 58.8 years; actual was 67. Trajectory matters as much as position — a model that cannot detect improvement rates will always lag on recovery stories.
−8.2 yrs
Actual: 67.0 · Predicted: 58.8
Singapore
Year 2006 · Outlier Efficiency
Underestimate
Exceptional governance and healthcare efficiency produces outcomes far beyond what GDP and schooling predict. Singapore achieves life expectancy of 87 years — the model predicted 79.7. This is not a data problem; it reflects that institutional quality and healthcare system efficiency are not captured by any variable in the WHO dataset.
−7.3 yrs
Actual: 87.0 · Predicted: 79.7
Saint Vincent & the Grenadines
Year 2001 · Small Island Volatility
Underestimate
Tiny populations mean one unusual year skews national statistics dramatically. The model trained on stable patterns from larger countries struggles with high-variance small states where a single disease outbreak or policy change can move the national average by several years.
−7.8 yrs
Actual: 79.0 · Predicted: 71.2
Uganda
Year 2013 · HIV/AIDS Policy Recovery
Underestimate
Uganda was an early and successful example of HIV/AIDS reduction through community health programmes — a policy response not captured by the indicators available. The model's HIV/AIDS variable reflects burden, not the response capability. Countries that rapidly deployed effective interventions consistently outperform the model's predictions.
−6.4 yrs
Actual: 67.0 · Predicted: 60.6
What these failures reveal. The model performs weakest where structural factors outside the dataset dominate — armed conflict, governance collapse, rapid policy-driven recovery, and exceptional institutional efficiency. These are not model failures in a technical sense; they are signals about what the WHO data cannot capture. Incorporating conflict indices, governance scores, and treatment programme data would likely close these gaps significantly for the countries that need accurate modelling most.
06 · Recommendations
What Should Come Next

For WHO, global health researchers, and data practitioners building on this work — the following directions would most meaningfully extend these findings.

For WHO Data Teams
Integrate Conflict and Fragility Indicators
The single largest prediction error — South Sudan 2000 at +11 years — reflects a complete absence of conflict data. The Fund for Peace Fragile States Index, ACLED conflict event data, and Uppsala Conflict Data Program records are all publicly available and would directly address the model's worst failure cases. A life expectancy model that cannot account for war is structurally limited precisely for the countries that need it most.
WHO · Global Health Observatory
For Health Researchers
Disaggregate HIV/AIDS from the General Model
HIV/AIDS accounts for 58.8% of the model's predictive power but affects only a concentrated subset of countries. A two-stage approach would be more rigorous: first classify countries by HIV/AIDS burden, then apply separate regression models to each group. The current model is essentially an HIV/AIDS model with other variables attached. Separating this signal would improve predictions for both high and low burden countries and produce more interpretable coefficients for every other variable.
Epidemiologists · Public Health Researchers
For Data Scientists
Add Temporal Structure to the Model
This analysis treated each country-year as an independent observation. A country's life expectancy in 2010 is not independent of its life expectancy in 2009. A longitudinal model using mixed-effects regression or an LSTM neural network would capture that trajectory and likely improve performance for transition cases like Zimbabwe and Uganda — where year-on-year change is the signal, not the absolute level.
Data Scientists · ML Practitioners
For Policy Analysts
Build Separate Models for Developed and Developing Countries
The distribution analysis shows that developed countries cluster tightly between 75 and 85 years while developing countries spread from under 40 to over 80. A single model trained on both groups explains two fundamentally different phenomena simultaneously. The variables predicting variation within developed countries — lifestyle factors, ageing dynamics, healthcare efficiency — differ from those driving variation in developing nations. Splitting the model would produce more actionable findings for policy in each context.
Policy Analysts · Global Health Institutions
For Future Research
Extend to 2016–2024 and Test COVID-19 Impact
This dataset ends in 2015. A model trained on 2000–2019 and validated against 2020–2023 WHO data would produce a genuine out-of-sample test of the model's durability. The COVID-19 pandemic caused the largest single-year drops in life expectancy since World War II in many countries. Understanding which variables predicted pre-pandemic life expectancy well, and where the pandemic broke those relationships, would be analytically and policy-relevant for building more resilient global health forecasting systems.
